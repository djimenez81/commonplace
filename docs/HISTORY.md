# History of the project

I have been using [Obsidian](https://obsidian.md/) as a note taking app for
a few years by now. Don't get me wrong, it is an amazing application. I have
tried a few others, and I have not found anything better, at least for my
use case. Indeed, it kinda has become my "_Life OS_", the most used program in
my computer, where my personal notes, my class notes (I am a university
mathematics professor), my task manager, my productivity system (_Multiple Scale
Planning_ based).

All this being said, there are things I do not like about Obsidian, or things
that I think could be done better.

- Regarding my task manager, implemented using the _tasks_ plugin, I dislike
that I cannot have sub-tasks, and that a task has to be fully contained within a
single line. Also, I don't totally love the aesthetics. 
- Obsidian has allowed me to make a centralized database of problems, solutions,
proofs, etc, that I have collected during 25 years of teaching. But _MarkDown_
is not _LaTeX_, and when I need to pass from one to the other, there is some
manual tweaking that has to be done in most cases.
- Continuing with _LaTeX_, I would find amazing to be able to edit and compile
_LaTeX_ projects directly from the same application where I take notes. And who
knows, it could be great if I could maybe use it also as my _Code Editor_.
- If you use _Dynalist Inc_ sync service (that I indeed use), syncing is indeed
seamless. But the _one-note-per-file_ philosophy, that has many advantages, also
means that you can easily get to thousands of files in your vault in a very
short amount of time. Most other cloud services (even those that are
self-hosted), take much more time syncing a lot of small files instead of fewer
larger ones.
- There are many reasons to segregate notes, but in Obsidian that means you need
different "vaults", that cannot interact (_e.g.:_ link) each other.
- Whether you use folders, _MoCs_ (maps of concept), tags, or some other
strategy, keeping a knowledge structure useful and consistent takes more effort
than it seems.

This made me think that I could try to write myself an app that would be
reasonably close to having most of the strengths I see in Obsidian, but it also
solves the weaknesses I find on it.

That is the reason why, in the last week of March 2026 I decided to start tying.
Of course, given my very limited experience coding, and the fact that this idea
seems complicated to implement, I do not expect this to be a short project, nor
free of failures. But you know what they say: **FAIL:** _First Attempt In
Learning_.
